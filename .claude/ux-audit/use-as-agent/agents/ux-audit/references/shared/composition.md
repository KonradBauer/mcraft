# Composition Frameworks

Expert frameworks for designing page narrative and emotional impact.
Used by: Wireframing (section sequencing, copy tone, emotional momentum).

---

## Emotional Design (Don Norman)

Three levels at which users emotionally engage with design. All three must be addressed — excelling at one while failing another creates dissonance.

**Visceral** — Immediate gut reaction to appearance. Sensory-driven. Users form positive or negative judgments before interacting.
- Wireframe role: Visual hierarchy, whitespace, typography weight, and aesthetic consistency set emotional tone before any reading occurs
- Annotate: Which sections carry visceral load (hero imagery, bold typography, color contrast)

**Behavioral** — Pleasure and effectiveness of use. Relates to usability, task completion, and feedback.
- Wireframe role: Navigation clarity, form simplicity, feedback mechanisms, predictable interaction patterns
- Annotate: Which elements must feel effortless (forms, navigation, CTAs)

**Reflective** — Long-term feelings about identity and values. Drives loyalty and advocacy.
- Wireframe role: Brand story, values representation, community signals, testimonials, "about" content
- Annotate: Which sections build lasting connection (brand narrative, social proof, mission statements)

**Key tension:** A beautifully designed site (visceral success) that's frustrating to navigate (behavioral failure) loses customers. A highly usable site that feels generic won't generate emotional connection that drives referrals.

## Hierarchy of User Needs (Aarron Walter)

Adapted from Maslow. Design must satisfy needs in order — but the top tier is the competitive differentiator:

1. **Functional** — It works
2. **Reliable** — It works consistently
3. **Usable** — It's easy to use
4. **Pleasurable** — It creates delight

Most teams stop at "usable" and never reach "pleasurable." Emotion is not a luxury layer — it's where differentiation happens.

**Wireframe application:** Annotate each component with the tier it primarily serves. Ensure every page has at least one element explicitly designed for the pleasurable tier — micro-interactions, personality in copy, unexpected moments of delight.

## Buyer Legends (Bryan Eisenberg)

Storytelling as business strategy. Maps what customers actually do (not what they should do) and turns the journey into compelling narratives.

**Process:**
1. Map the customer's actual behavior and decision path
2. Identify narrative moments — where beliefs shift, doubts arise, confidence builds
3. Align brand intent with customer reality across all touchpoints
4. Surface what's working, what's broken, and why buyers bail

**Brand Diamond:** Identifies core identity, quirks, and vulnerabilities — the human elements that create real connection. In an era of AI-generated generic content, authentic storytelling becomes the competitive advantage.

**Wireframe application:**
Each page annotated with three narrative markers:
- **Entry belief:** What does the visitor believe when arriving at this page?
- **Required shift:** What mental or emotional change must occur?
- **Exit belief:** What should they believe when leaving?

Copy and visual hierarchy reinforce the unique brand story, not generic category patterns.

## Emotional Targeting (Talia Wolf)

Methodology built on 223 emotional triggers that impact purchasing decisions. The core insight: people buy based on emotional outcomes, then rationalize with logic.

**Process:**
1. Research the customer's emotional pain points and desired outcomes
2. Identify emotional triggers that drive their decisions (not features)
3. Design experiences where customers can see and feel the outcomes they desire
4. Test based on emotional resonance, not element-level tweaks

**Key questions for every section:**
- Am I making this about the company, or about the customer?
- Can visitors immediately see and feel the emotional outcomes they desire?
- Am I using emotion in both copy and visuals?

**Evidence:** For a client with a 3,000-word landing page, Wolf didn't shorten it — she added more words expressing target audience pain points and communicating emotional outcomes. Result: 41% increase in signups, 15% revenue boost.

**Wireframe application:**
- Tag each section with the emotional trigger it activates
- Copy articulates both rational benefits and emotional outcomes (not "Cloud backup storage" but "Never lose a memory, never panic at 2am wondering if your photos are safe")
- Evaluate pages for emotional momentum — do they progressively deepen engagement or plateau?

## Narrative Arc for Pages

Pages tell stories — both within a single page (narrative arc) and across the site journey. A wireframe can have perfect components and correct hierarchy yet feel flat because it lacks narrative momentum.

### Single-Page Arc

Every page follows a four-part structure:

1. **Hook/Hero** — Capture attention, set context. Reframe the visitor's problem as a solvable opportunity
2. **Tension/Problem** — Surface the user's challenge or desired outcome. Create emotional stakes
3. **Evidence/Development** — Proof, case studies, details, alternatives. Build confidence through specifics
4. **Resolution/CTA** — Clear path forward, action invitation. The climax of the page's story

**Story roles:**
- User is the hero (not the brand)
- Brand is the guide (mentor, facilitator)
- Structure: Problem → Plan → Action → Success

### Cross-Page Coherence

Page-to-page transitions annotated with information scent cues — what link labels, CTAs, and contextual signals tell the user about what comes next.

Cross-page messaging mapped to buying stages:
- **Awareness** → "I have this problem"
- **Consideration** → "These solutions exist"
- **Decision** → "This is the right choice for me"

No narrative leap left unbridged. Each page propels to the next.

**Wireframe application:**
Each page includes a narrative annotation layer:
```
SECTION: [name]
- Narrative role: hook | tension | evidence | resolution
- Entry belief: "[what visitor believes arriving here]"
- Emotional shift: From [state A] → [state B]
- Exit belief: "[what visitor should believe leaving]"
- Momentum: propels forward | resolves | plateaus (fix)
```

## Persuasion Architecture (FAST — Patrick Campbell)

Structures persuasion around four pillars. Developed for pricing pages but applies to any page requiring a discrete action:

- **Focus** — Eliminate distractions. Center on the value proposition. Single hero statement, minimal competing navigation, whitespace guiding the eye
- **Amplify confidence** — Social proof, trust signals, ROI messaging, guarantees positioned strategically
- **Shape packaging** — Structure options around buyer segments, not feature lists. Persona-specific value propositions
- **Trigger action** — Clear CTAs with visual contrast, pre-selected defaults, urgency signals where authentic

**Wireframe application:** Annotate each conversion-oriented page with FAST dimensions. Verify that Focus eliminates noise, Amplify addresses specific anxieties, Shape matches audience segments, and Trigger creates clear action paths.

---

## Integration Sequence

When composing a page wireframe, apply frameworks in this order:

1. **Foundation (Norman + Walter):** Determine visceral/behavioral/reflective appeal. Ensure all four need tiers are addressed
2. **Narrative Structure (Arc):** Define hook → tension → evidence → resolution sections
3. **Emotional Design (Emotional Targeting + Buyer Legends):** Map emotional triggers per section. Annotate entry/exit beliefs
4. **Persuasion Mechanics (FAST):** For conversion-oriented pages, verify focus, confidence, packaging, and action triggers
5. **Cross-Page Coherence:** Annotate how this page bridges to the next. Verify buying-stage alignment
