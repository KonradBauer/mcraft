# B2B Services: Discovery

## Key Assumptions to Form

### 1. Service Archetype

Which strategic archetype fits the firm's positioning?

- **Credibility-Led** — leads with thought leadership, credentials, intellectual authority (consultancies, law firms, advisory)
- **Showcase-Led** — leads with portfolio and project quality (creative agencies, design firms, architecture studios)
- **Methodology-Led** — leads with proprietary process or framework (implementation firms, process consultancies)
- **Relationship-Led** — leads with team depth and cultural fit (boutique advisory, executive coaching, recruitment)

*Many firms are hybrids. Identify the primary and note the secondary.*

### 2. Team Size and Structure

Directly determines site architecture complexity:

| Size | Architecture implication |
|------|------------------------|
| Solo practitioner (1–2) | Single-page or minimal site; founder IS the brand |
| Boutique (3–15) | Multi-page; full bios on single team page; leadership prominent |
| Mid-size (15–50) | Filterable team grid; individual profile pages; practice area pages |
| Large firm (50+) | Searchable directory; sub-sites per practice; industry landing pages |

### 3. Specialization Breadth

How narrow is the firm's focus? This is the single most consequential positioning decision.

- **Horizontal generalist** — expert in a discipline (e.g., "UX Design") across industries
- **Vertical specialist** — expert in an industry (e.g., "Healthcare consulting") across disciplines
- **Niche expert** — narrow intersection (e.g., "HIPAA compliance for health-tech startups")

Research shows 77% of professional practices specialize in one or more industries. Fear of insufficient work turns specialists into undifferentiated generalists — the website must reflect the firm's actual positioning, not hedge toward a broader audience.

*Assess with the "5-second specificity test": would a visitor know exactly what the firm does and for whom from the hero headline alone?*

### 4. Referral vs. Cold Traffic Ratio

Fundamentally different site architectures serve these audiences:

| Traffic type | What they need | CVR benchmark |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Warm referral** | Quick validation — team, credentials, relevant case studies. Already pre-qualified. | ~2.9% |
| **Organic search** | Educational content first, progressive trust-building, thought leadership | ~2.7% |
| **Paid search** | Immediate clarity, competitive differentiation, conversion-optimized landing | ~1.5–3.2% |

Professional services sites achieve 4–10% visitor-to-lead conversion overall (vs. ~1.1% for SaaS) — largely because of referral-heavy traffic profiles and shorter paths to consultation booking.

*Key question: Is this a referral-driven business that needs a validation site, or a growth-stage firm that needs to generate cold leads?*

### 5. Typical Engagement Value

Affects site sophistication expectations and conversion design:

- **< $5K projects** — high-volume, low-touch. Site must self-qualify efficiently. Pricing transparency expected.
- **$5K–$50K engagements** — mid-touch. Site needs robust proof (case studies, methodology). Pricing ranges acceptable.
- **$50K+ strategic engagements** — high-touch. Site exists to earn the first meeting, not close. Thought leadership and team credibility dominate.

### 6. Industry Verticals Served

Which industries represent significant revenue? Each vertical with >15% of revenue warrants a dedicated industry expertise page with:
- Vertical-specific case studies
- Team members with sector experience
- Regulatory/compliance awareness
- Industry-specific terminology and process adaptations

*Multi-vertical firms need filtering and self-selection paths. Single-vertical firms can embed industry context throughout.*

### 7. Competitive Positioning Strategy

Apply the "Waterfall of Differentiation" framework:
1. **Category** — what kind of firm? (e.g., "Digital Agency")
2. **Specialization** — what's the narrow focus? (e.g., "SaaS Product Marketing")
3. **Qualifiers** — what secondary filters apply? (e.g., "B2B companies $5M–$50M ARR")

Each level narrows the competitive set. A website that fails to establish this hierarchy forces visitors to parse vague, interchangeable claims.

*Anti-pattern: "Comprehensive solutions" and "industry-leading expertise" — language that feels safe but differentiates nothing.*

### 8. Thought Leadership Investment Level

The #1 marketing priority for high-growth professional services firms. 2× marketing investment in research/content correlates with 4× growth.

- **None/minimal** — no blog, no publications → site relies entirely on referral validation
- **Moderate** — regular articles, occasional whitepapers → needs a basic Insights section
- **Heavy** — original research, speaking, publications, media → needs full Thought Leadership Hub with pillar page architecture (3,000–5,000+ word guides with supporting cluster content)

*Discovery question: What intellectual property has the firm published? What's the publishing cadence?*

### 9. Buyer Psychology Type

Which referral typology dominates the firm's pipeline? Determines what proof to front-load:

| Referral type | Driver | What to front-load on site |
|--------------|--------|---------------------------|
| **Experience-based** | Direct knowledge of firm's work quality | Client success stories, post-engagement outcomes, support quality |
| **Reputation-based** | Market awareness, prestige | Tier-1 client logos above fold, awards, media mentions |
| **Expertise-based** | Visible published knowledge on a specific problem | Deep content hubs, published methodology, team SME credentials |

Research: 81.5% of firms receive referrals from people who were never their clients. 34.7% of referral factors tie directly to visible expertise. A high-quality website is a prerequisite — referral sources routinely rule out firms based on poor digital presentation alone.

### 10. Geographic Scope

| Scope | Site implication |
|-------|-----------------|
| Local (single city) | Local trust signals, community engagement, local SEO |
| Regional (multi-city) | Office locations, regional team highlights |
| National | Industry-focused rather than geography-focused |
| International | Language/currency handling, regional team pages, time zone awareness |

### 11. Pricing Transparency Expectations

Controversial in services — research shows transparency qualifies leads, but many firms resist.

| Level | Description | Impact |
|-------|------------|--------|
| **Level 1** | Pricing page with cost drivers and typical ranges | Top 10% for trust |
| **Level 2** | Pricing videos explaining what influences costs per service | Top 3% for trust |
| **Level 3** | Interactive pricing estimator tool | Up to 300% more leads |
| **Level 4** | Exact prices displayed | Maximum trust; not feasible for most custom work |

Most agencies and consultancies are best served with ranges, starting prices, or tiers. Full opacity ("contact us for pricing") creates maximum friction — buyers can't build internal business cases or compare alternatives.

*Discovery question: Is the firm's service productizable (flat rates) or fully custom (ranges only)? What's the firm's comfort level with public pricing?*

## Common Project Shapes

| Shape | Typical scope | Default archetype | Key pages |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Solo consultant** | One-pager or minimal site | Credibility-Led | Home (with bio, services, proof), Contact |
| **Boutique agency** | Multi-page (5–10 pages) | Showcase-Led or Methodology-Led | Home, Services, Work/Portfolio, Team, Contact |
| **Mid-size firm** | Multi-page with practice areas (10–25 pages) | Credibility-Led with practice areas | Home, Services (per practice), Team, Case Studies, Insights, Industries, Contact |
| **Large professional firm** | Multi-page + sub-sites per practice (25+ pages) | Credibility-Led with full content architecture | Home, Practice Areas (with sub-pages), Industry Pages, Team Directory, Thought Leadership Hub, Case Study Library, Careers, Contact |

## Unique Constraints

### Thought leadership as conversion engine
Thought leadership is the #1 conversion mechanism for high-growth professional services firms. Unlike SaaS (where product access drives conversion), services firms convert through demonstrated expertise. This means the Insights/Resources section is not a "nice to have" — for growth-stage firms, it's the primary lead generation mechanism.

### Team pages are high-traffic
Team/People pages are typically the 2nd most-visited pages after the homepage. Visitors are evaluating whether they want to work with these specific humans. Under-investing in team pages is a direct conversion leak.

### Bifurcated proof requirement
The buying committee includes both analytical buyers (procurement, technical leads) and executive sponsors. The site needs two distinct proof formats:
- **Case Studies** — structured, metrics-driven, ROI-focused (for procurement path)
- **Success Stories** — narrative-driven, relationship-focused, emotionally resonant (for executive path)

Sites that only offer one format lose half the committee.

### The brochure trap
The most common failure: treating the website as a digital brochure instead of a conversion tool. A brochure describes the firm. A conversion tool earns the first meeting. Every page should have a clear next step — either deeper exploration or consultation booking.

### Design-capability mismatch
Design quality signals work quality. A firm charging $50K+ for consulting with a website that looks like it was built on a free platform in 2015 creates an immediate credibility gap. This constraint is stronger for services than any other B2B category because the service itself is intangible — the website is often the only tangible artifact a prospect evaluates.
