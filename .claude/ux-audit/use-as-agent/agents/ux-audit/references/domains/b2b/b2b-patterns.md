# B2B: Page Layout Patterns

Domain knowledge shared across all B2B site types.
Part of: references/domains/b2b/

---

Select a pattern based on the Market Maturity and Product Complexity.

**Narrative arc: Trigger → Guide → Outcome** — All page layouts follow this three-phase structure:
- **Trigger**: Hook the visitor by naming their situation, pain, or goal (Hero, Problem Agitation)
- **Guide**: Build credibility and show the solution path (Features, Process, Proof)
- **Outcome**: Close with a specific CTA that feels like the natural conclusion of the preceding copy

---

## A. The Innovation Layout (How-To)
**Use when**: Product is novel, category is unknown, or requires explanation.
**Core Narrative**: "You are struggling with X (Old Way). We maintain the state of X better (New Way)."
**Focus**: Problem/Solution education.
**Structure**:
1. **Hero**: Problem statement + New capability.
2. **Comparison**: "The Old Way" (Pain) vs "The New Way" (Solution).
3. **How It Works**: 3-Step Process breakdown.
4. **Benefits**: Outcome-focused list.
5. **Proof**: Early adopters/Beta users.

---

## B. The Platform Layout (Networker)
**Use when**: Product serves multiple distinct personas or industries (Horizontal Platform).
**Core Narrative**: "One platform for all your [Industry] needs."
**Focus**: Routing users to their specific use case.
**Structure**:
1. **Hero**: Broad value proposition.
2. **The Router**: Grid/Selector ("I am a...", "I need to...").
3. **Use Cases**: Section per key persona.
4. **Integration Bar**: "Works with [Tool A, Tool B]".

---

## C. The Competitor Layout (Differentiator)
**Use when**: Market is mature, users know the category but need a reason to switch.
**Focus**: Comparison and specific advantages.
**Structure**:
1. **Hero**: Direct differentiator ("The fastest...", "The only...").
2. **Comparison Table**: "Us vs Them" (Feature/Benefit matrix).
3. **Switching Logic**: "Migration is easy" (Reduce fear of switching).
4. **Social Proof**: Leader badges (e.g., "Rated #1").

---

## D. Pricing Page
**Use when:** Product has tiered plans; visitor is solution-aware or product-aware; conversion goal is plan selection (self-serve) or sales contact (enterprise).
**Core Narrative:** "Here's exactly what you get at each level — the right plan exists for your stage, and you can start without risk."
**Focus:** Reducing decision anxiety through clear structure and anchoring.
**Structure:**
1. **Billing toggle + tier cards** (above fold) — Annual/monthly toggle with anchor pricing. 3 tiers default; middle tier carries "Most Popular" badge (decoy effect increases mid-tier selection by 12–15%). Each card: plan name, price, one-line description, 5–7 feature highlights, primary CTA. Enterprise shows "Contact Sales."
2. **Feature comparison table** — Full feature matrix below cards. Checkmarks for boolean, specific values for quantitative. Highlighted column matches recommended tier. Mobile: horizontal scroll or stacked accordion per tier.
3. **Pricing-specific social proof** — Customer quotes referencing plan tier or ROI. "We started on Starter and upgraded to Pro within 3 months."
4. **FAQ section** — Pricing objections: billing changes, refund policy, usage limits, upgrade/downgrade process.
5. **Final CTA** — Repeats primary conversion action. Never place "Free Trial" and "Book Demo" at equal visual weight — one must be primary.

**Variant logic:** PLG: emphasize self-serve CTA + free tier. Sales-led: de-emphasize specific pricing, elevate "Talk to Sales." Freemium: clear "Free forever" label.

---

## E. Case Study / Success Story
**Use when:** Visitor is product-aware or most-aware, evaluating vendor credibility; conversion goal is confidence for sales conversation or trial signup.
**Core Narrative:** "A company like yours had this problem, chose us, and achieved these specific results."
**Focus:** Specificity and relatability.
**Structure:**
1. **Result-first headline** (above fold) — Not "How Company X Improved Workflows" but "How Company X Cut Onboarding from 14 Days to 2." Include company logo, industry tag, single hero metric.
2. **Challenge section** — 2–3 paragraphs describing the client's pre-product situation. Name specific pain points the reader likely shares.
3. **Approach/Solution section** — How the product was implemented: features used, timeline, customization. Does product education through the lens of a real deployment.
4. **Results with metrics** — 3–5 quantified outcomes with context (% improvement, absolute numbers, time saved). Present as a stats block. Include source attribution.
5. **Pull quote** — Single compelling quote from a named stakeholder (title + company). Place after results — validates the metrics.
6. **CTA section** — "See how [Product] can deliver similar results." If industry-specific, route to industry-specific landing page.

**What converts vs. informs:** Vague outcomes ("improved efficiency") inform. Quantified, attributed, time-bound outcomes ("reduced processing time by 62% in 90 days") convert.

## F. Feature / Solution Subpage
**Use when:** Product has multiple features or use cases, each serving a distinct persona or solving a distinct problem.
**Core Narrative:** "Here's the specific problem this feature solves, how it works, who uses it, and proof it works."
**Focus:** Depth on a single capability.
**Structure:**
1. **Problem headline + subhead** (above fold) — Not "Our Analytics Dashboard" but "Stop guessing which campaigns drive revenue."
2. **How it works** — 3–4 step walkthrough with annotated screenshots. Each step: action verb headline + sentence + visual.
3. **Who uses it** — 2–3 persona-specific use cases with outcome statements.
4. **Feature-specific proof** — Testimonial from a user of *this specific feature*, not generic product social proof.
5. **Integration/compatibility** — 3–5 most relevant integrations (not the full grid).
6. **Pricing callout or CTA** — Clarify which plan includes this feature. Route to pricing or trial with feature highlighted.

---

## G. Comparison / vs. Page
**Use when:** Visitor is a competitor-switcher — product-aware, arriving via "[Product] vs [Competitor]" queries. These pages convert at 10–20% vs. 1–5% for top-of-funnel content.
**Core Narrative:** "Here's an honest comparison — and here's why switching makes sense for your use case."
**Focus:** Honest differentiation and switching anxiety reduction.
**Structure:**
1. **Verdict summary** (above fold) — 2–3 sentence honest comparison. Not "We're better at everything" but "If you need X, choose us. If you need Y, [Competitor] may be better."
2. **Comparison table** — Feature-by-feature matrix. Checkmarks for boolean, values for quantitative, text for qualitative. Highlight own product column. Include pricing row. Update quarterly.
3. **Reasons to switch** — 3–5 differentiators with proof points. Frame as "What you gain by switching."
4. **Objection handling** — Top 3 switching anxieties: data migration, learning curve, contract/cost. Include specific migration support details.
5. **Switcher social proof** — Testimonials specifically from customers who switched from this competitor.
6. **Migration CTA** — "Start your free migration" or "Talk to our migration team" — not generic "Start Trial."

**What converts:** Honesty. Pages that concede competitor strengths and explain own strengths convert because they match the visitor's mental model.

## H. About / Company Page
**Use when:** Visitor is evaluating vendor credibility — common in Authority-Led archetypes, regulated industries, enterprise sales.
**Core Narrative:** "We're real people who built this for a reason you'll relate to — and credible organizations trust us."
**Focus:** Human connection and stability signals.
**Structure:**
1. **Origin story** (above fold) — 2–3 sentences on why the company was founded. Focus on the problem that motivated founders.
2. **Mission/values** — 3–5 concrete values tied to observable behaviors. Not "We value innovation" but "We ship weekly and share our roadmap publicly."
3. **Team grid** — Photos, names, titles, one humanizing detail per person. Companies >30: leadership team + link to full page.
4. **Press/investors/milestones** — Funding, media logos, awards, key milestones. Serves enterprise buyer's need for stability signals.
5. **Hiring CTA** — Second-most-visited page by job candidates. Include "Join Us" section with example open roles.

**Conversion goal:** Does not convert directly — supports conversion by reducing trust deficit. CTA should be contextual: enterprise → case study; job seekers → careers; press → media kit. Single "Contact Us" is insufficient for diverse About page intent.

## I. Enterprise Landing Page
**Use when:** Product serves enterprise buyers (ACV >$25K), sales cycle is 3–12 months, buying committee includes 6–10 stakeholders, conversion goal is "Talk to Sales."
**Core Narrative:** "We understand enterprise complexity — here's proof we can handle your scale, security, and compliance requirements."
**Focus:** Trust-first (inverts the standard trust hierarchy).
**Structure:**
1. **Enterprise-specific hero** (above fold) — Headline addresses enterprise pain ("Secure, scalable, built for teams of 500+"). CTA is "Talk to Sales" or "Request a Demo" — never "Start Free Trial." Named enterprise logo adjacent to CTA.
2. **Security and compliance block** — SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR, ISO 27001 badges with links to detailed documentation. Appears higher on enterprise pages than SMB — security is a gating criterion.
3. **Named customer references** — "Trusted by [Specific Enterprise]" with permission-based logos. Enterprise buyers want companies of similar size and industry, not volume counts.
4. **Enterprise-specific features** — SSO, SCIM, audit logs, RBAC, custom SLAs, dedicated CSM. Presented as a checklist — the buyer is checking boxes against procurement requirements.
5. **ROI/business case section** — Quantified outcomes from enterprise deployments. "Average 340% ROI over 3 years" with methodology link. Arms the internal champion to build the business case.
6. **Implementation and support** — Dedicated CSM, onboarding timeline, integration support, training. Post-sale experience matters as much as product capabilities.
7. **Contact form** — 6–8 fields (company size, use case, timeline). "What brings you here today?" dropdown for lead routing. No chatbot as primary — enterprise buyers expect human contact.

## J. RFQ Form Anatomy

**Use when:** any B2B site where a visitor needs to request a quote rather than (or in addition to) placing a direct order.

### Catalog / Configured-Product RFQ
Pre-populated fields (from product page context):
* SKU / part number, configuration selections, base specifications

User-entered fields:
* Quantity (with real-time price update if instant pricing is available)
* Delivery timeline (dropdown: ASAP / 2 weeks / 1 month / 3 months / Flexible)
* Ship-to location (address or ZIP for freight estimate)
* PO / budget code (optional)
* Notes / special requirements

Output: Instant price (if within capability bounds) OR Quote Cart entry (if manual quoting required)

### Custom / Engineered-to-Order RFQ

| Field group | Fields | Input type |
|-------------|--------|------------|
| **Geometric Definition** | CAD file upload | WebGL drag-and-drop zone (STEP, SLDPRT, STL, DXF) |
| **Material & Process** | Material family, specific alloy, process | Dependent dropdowns |
| **Secondary Operations** | Tapping, bending, welding, anodizing, plating, painting | Toggle group |
| **Commercial Terms** | Quantity (with volume tiers), delivery date, shipping location | Numeric steppers + date picker |
| **Compliance & QA** | ITAR, AS9100, RoHS, REACH, PPAP, FAI — per line item | Granular checkboxes |
| **Context** | Application description, project name, budget range (optional) | Textarea + text inputs |

**Key principle:** Never show a blank RFQ form when context is available. If visitor arrived from a product page, configurator, or instant quote flow, pre-populate all available fields before presenting the form.

## K. Dual CTA Hierarchy Pattern

**Use when:** page or section has two conversion actions with different buyer intents — one for immediate purchase/engagement, one for longer-cycle consideration (RFQ, custom quote, sales contact).

### Visual Hierarchy Rules
* **Primary CTA** — represents the dominant use case for this page. Filled button, high contrast, full width or prominent placement.
* **Secondary CTA** — outline or text link, positioned below or beside primary. Not buried in footer.
* **Contextual labels** — CTAs must describe what happens, not just "Contact Us":
  * ✅ "Request Quote for Modified Version"
  * ✅ "Get Custom Quote for This Part"
  * ✅ "Talk to an Engineer"
  * ❌ "Contact Us"
  * ❌ "Learn More"

### Pre-Populated Context Rule
Any CTA that opens an RFQ or contact form must carry context from the current page:
* From a product page → pre-fill: part number, configuration, spec selections
* From a configurator → pre-fill: process, material, quantity, uploaded file
* From a quote review → pre-fill: all quote parameters

### Quote Cart Pattern
For sites with complex RFQ workflows: the Quote Cart mirrors the B2B cart UI but accumulates RFQ line items rather than purchase items. "Submit Request for Quote" replaces "Place Order." This allows multi-line RFQ submissions and mirrors the familiar cart interaction model for buyers who know the cart pattern.

**Anti-pattern:** Two equal-weight filled buttons. Forces buyer to stop and read both options before acting. One primary action, one secondary — always.

## Gating Strategy Principles

B2B content gating must balance lead capture against evaluation friction. The principle: ungate everything needed for evaluation, gate content that signals depth commitment.

**Ungated:** Product/service specs, pricing guidance, case study summaries, methodology overviews, comparison content — anything a buyer needs to decide "is this worth a conversation?"

**Gated:** Full research reports, detailed ROI analyses, implementation guides, recorded webinars, tools/calculators that require personalization — content that signals the visitor has moved past evaluation into active consideration.

**Domain manifestations:**
- b2b-saas: Ungated product tours and docs; gated ROI calculators and enterprise whitepapers
- b2b-services: Ungated methodology and team; gated diagnostic tools and detailed case studies
- b2b-industrial: Ungated specs and datasheets; gated CAD bundles and custom configuration saves

## Audit Checklists

### Conversion Factors Audit (The 6 Drivers)
Before freezing a wireframe, check these 6 factors:

1. **Value Proposition**: Is it clear *what* enables the benefit?
2. **Relevance**: Does the content match the user's source/intent?
3. **Clarity**: Is the visual hierarchy obvious? (Squint test).
4. **Urgency**: Is there a reason to act *now*? (Limited time, loss aversion).
5. **Anxiety**: What is the user afraid of? (Security, difficulty, cost). Have we addressed it?
6. **Distraction**: Are there elements drawing attention away from the Goal? (Remove them).

### Mobile Viability Check
* **Tables**: Must describe mobile fallback (e.g., "Scrollable horizontal" or "Cards").
* **Forms**: Single column only.
* **Touch Targets**: CTAs must be prominent and full-width on mobile.

### Navigation Quality Check
* **Desktop navigation visible** — no hamburger menu on desktop (hides options, increases interaction cost).
* **Mega-menu under 30 links** — more options = slower decisions = higher bounce (Hick's Law).
* **Outcome-specific CTA** — not "Contact Us" (process CTA) but "Get a Demo of [X]" (outcome CTA).

### Page Quality Check
* **Homepage as router, not brochure** — routes visitors to right destination by awareness state, doesn't list every feature.
* **Case study titles quantified** — "How Company X Cut Onboarding from 14 Days to 2" not "How Company X Improved Workflows."
* **Single primary CTA per section** — two CTAs at equal visual weight = decision paralysis. One primary, one secondary (ghost button).

### Copy Quality Check
* **Headline clarity test** — Can a first-time visitor state what the company does from the H1 alone?
* **CTA specificity test** — "Get Started" = fail (generic). "Start Your Free Trial" = pass. "Book a 30-Min Walkthrough" = strong.
* **Social proof specificity test** — "Used by thousands" = fail. "Used by 12,000+ teams at Shopify, Stripe, and Figma" = pass.
