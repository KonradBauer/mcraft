## Shopping Intent Model

E-commerce visitors operate on a shopping intent spectrum, not a B2B awareness funnel.

| Shopper Type | Mental State | Primary Need | Conversion Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Product-focused** | Knows exact item, wants speed | Quick search → confirm → buy | Fast checkout, product match confirmation |
| **Researcher** | Comparing options, collecting info | Detailed specs, reviews, comparison | Trust + information completeness |
| **Browser** | Leisure/inspiration, no purchase intent | Discovery, what's new, trending | Emotional impulse, scarcity, deals |
| **Bargain hunter** | Seeking best deal | Sale items, discounts, coupons | Price advantage, savings display |
| **One-time shopper** | Unfamiliar with brand, often gift buyer | Clear nav, guest checkout, trust signals | Frictionless guest checkout |

### Product-Focused Shopper
Knows exactly what they want. Arrives via product-specific search or direct link. Zero tolerance for browsing — needs search bar, quick navigation, fast-loading PDP. Conversion depends on confirming product match and enabling fast checkout.

### Researcher
Evaluating options across multiple tabs and brands. Reads every review, checks specifications, compares prices. Needs complete product information, customer photos, sizing guides, comparison tools. Will leave if information feels incomplete.

### Browser
No purchase intent on arrival. Scrolling through social media led them here, or they're killing time. Responds to visual merchandising, curated collections, trending items. Converts through emotional impulse — a product that triggers "I want that" — combined with scarcity ("Only 3 left") or deals.

### Bargain Hunter
Motivated primarily by deals. Goes straight to Sale section, searches for promo codes, compares prices across sites. Converts when the discount feels significant and exclusive. Risk: attracts low-LTV customers if over-indexed.

### One-Time Shopper
First visit, often buying a gift or one-off purchase. Unfamiliar with the brand, needs reassurance the site is legitimate. Guest checkout is essential — forced account creation causes 34% cart abandonment.

## Conversion Triggers

| Trigger | Mechanism | Where it appears |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Social proof volume | Products with 5+ reviews are 270% more likely to sell | PDP review section, product cards |
| Scarcity signals | "Only 3 left in stock" / "Selling fast" | PDP, product cards |
| Free shipping threshold | "Add $23 more for free shipping!" | Cart sidebar, announcement bar |
| Risk reversal | "30-day hassle-free returns" / "Money-back guarantee" | Trust bar, PDP micro-copy |
| Urgency (time-limited) | Flash sales, countdown timers, promo code expiry | Hero, announcement bar, PDP |
| Visual proof | Customer photos, lifestyle imagery, video | PDP gallery, UGC section |

## "Coming from X" — Cross-Domain Warnings

When an e-commerce project team has experience primarily in other site types, these transfer errors are common:

| Coming from... | Watch for... |
|----------------|--------------|
| **B2B SaaS** | Over-explaining, under-selling. E-commerce visitors don't need feature education — they need to see, want, trust, and buy. Kill the "How It Works" section unless the product genuinely requires explanation. Don't gate anything behind forms. |
| **Portfolio** | Under-investing in product photography. Portfolio sites showcase finished work; e-commerce needs multiple angles, lifestyle context, scale reference, and customer photos. One hero image per product is not enough. |
| **Media / Content** | Prioritizing content over commerce. Blog content supports SEO but the site's job is to sell products. Don't let editorial sections compete with shopping paths. |
| **Nonprofit** | Appealing to values without enabling action. Sustainable/ethical brands still need clear product pages and frictionless checkout — mission statements don't replace "Add to Cart." |
| **Local Business** | Assuming all visitors are nearby. E-commerce serves a global audience; location-based trust signals (Google reviews, maps) are irrelevant. Product reviews and return policies replace proximity trust. |
